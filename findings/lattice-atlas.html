THE MESH

The methodology has been applied to the Taylors Arm flying fox infestation. The mesh holds every connection revealed through that application. Each connection listed below is structural — present in the fabric of the evidence, not drawn by this analysis.

Domain axis

Flying fox governance failure operating across local government, state legislation, Commonwealth legislation, and biosecurity law simultaneously.

Time axis

A minimum 37-month documented response void. March 2023 to April 2026. Colony described as having more than doubled prior to that first documented date. The void is not silence — it is a structured absence producing an evidence record of its own.

Person axis

A named community member, Vicki Provost, who formally addressed her elected council on 16 March 2023 with five documented categories of harm, one specific and legally grounded remedy request, and received no institutional response of any kind across the entire documented period.

Cost direction axis

Cost flows entirely toward the community. The flying fox colony imposes contaminated drinking water, soiled property, vehicle damage, loss of outdoor space, and commercial disruption to a nationally known tourism asset. No cost is borne by the institution. Non-response is cost-free for council.

Information flow axis

Council holds the knowledge of the complaint from 16 March 2023. The community holds the knowledge of the harm from November 2019 or earlier. The grant mechanism exists and is known to council — it used it in 2017. The camp registration mechanism exists and is publicly notified. None of this information has crossed into institutional action.

Accountability direction axis

Accountability flows in one direction only — toward the community seeking it. The community carries the cost of attempting accountability. The institution bears no consequence for non-response.

Legislative basis axis

Three instruments converge on the same trees in the same village: the Biosecurity Act 2015 (Camphor Laurel as priority weed requiring council action), the Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016 (grey-headed flying fox as vulnerable species requiring protection), and the EPBC Act 1999 (federal vulnerable listing requiring referral for significant impact). These three instruments produce a regulatory intersection requiring navigation — not an absence of authority.

Geographic axis

The failure operates across a regional corridor. Bellbrook sits in Kempsey Shire. Taylors Arm sits in Nambucca Valley. Both sit within the same hinterland river ecology. Both sit within the same state electorate (Oxley). Both sit within the same federal electorate (Cowper). The geographic adjacency means the failure is not a local anomaly — it is a corridor pattern.

Population axis

The population bearing cost is a village of 133 people without reticulated water, without council management response, without electoral advocacy, and without Commonwealth monitoring registration confirmed for their camp.

Mechanism axis

Seven structural harm mechanisms from the catalogue are confirmed active in this mesh: Silence and Non-Response (Mechanism 11), Absence-as-Evidence (operative from 16 March 2023), Documented Compliance as Shield (Mechanism 9 — the away meeting is claimed as an achievement while the substance of the engagement is absent from all records), Time as Structural Harm Mechanism (Mechanism 10 — 37 months), Accountability Cost Externalisation (Mechanism 46 — the community bears all cost of accountability-seeking), Institutional Memory Break (non-canonical to the catalogue but confirmed active — the Outstanding Actions failure is a structural mechanism), and Compliant Process Harm (Mechanism 8 — every council procedure was followed; the harm continued).

Institutional actor axis

Four actors present in the evidence: Nambucca Valley Council (primary — non-responsive); Kempsey Shire Council (adjacent — partially responsive, denied on budget grounds); the NSW state government (grant mechanism exists but no documented uptake for hinterland); and the Commonwealth (EPBC framework exists, monitoring program exists, camp not confirmed registered).

Self-detection axis

This investigation is applying the same standard to institutional non-response that MAYHEM applies in every case. The mesh detects its own operation: it cannot make the community's drinking water clean. It can make the failure to address it structurally visible and formally documented.

EMERGENT PATTERN REGISTER

Mandatory output under GOVFAIL Enforcement 5.

Emergent Pattern 1 — The Camphor Laurel Regulatory Pinch

Surfaces generating this pattern: Mesh 1 (community harm — specific request for Camphor Laurel removal) and Mesh 3 (legislative architecture — Camphor Laurel as mandatory biosecurity obligation AND as flying fox habitat requiring EPBC assessment).

What this pattern reveals that path-level analysis cannot: The community's remedy request identified, without knowing it, the exact point where the mandatory biosecurity obligation and the discretionary flying fox protection framework intersect. Vicki Provost did not ask council to disperse the colony. She asked council to remove the trees. That ask was simultaneously: the most legally grounded request available (mandatory weed obligation, no EPBC referral required for weed removal per se); the most structurally complex request available (well-established Camphor Laurels require a Review of Environmental Factors under the EP&A Act before removal, as confirmed by Kempsey Shire's April 2024 process); and the most tractable request available (Kempsey Shire navigated it successfully in 2024, the model exists). No single path analysis surfaces this three-way intersection. The community found it instinctively. The institution did not engage with it at all.

Structural significance: HIGH. The regulatory pinch is the key to unlocking the case. If the Camphor Laurel trees are addressed through a Review of Environmental Factors process — as Kempsey Shire demonstrated is possible — the colony's roosting habitat is reduced without requiring EPBC referral for camp dispersal. This is the pathway that was never taken.

Emergent Pattern 2 — The Institutional Memory Trap

Surfaces generating this pattern: Mesh 2 (institutional non-response — Outstanding Actions register contains no entry) and Mesh 3 (governance architecture — mayoral transition October 2024, new council has no institutional knowledge of the matter).

What this pattern reveals that path-level analysis cannot: The Outstanding Actions register is not merely a tracking tool. It is the institutional memory transfer mechanism between successive councils. Its failure in this case is not a passive omission. It is an active structural consequence: the new council under Mayor Gary Lee has no formal knowledge that the complaint was ever made. Any future advocacy to the current council begins from zero — not from a position of building on a prior request, but from the beginning of a fresh case with no institutional history. The community's March 2023 complaint has been institutionally erased not by decision but by administrative silence. This is Mechanism 11 (Silence and Non-Response) and Mechanism 54 (Reform as Reset) in simultaneous operation — except here the reset was not triggered by a reform but by an election and the structural gap in the tracking system.

Structural significance: HIGH. Every future advocacy effort must account for this. The first step in any submission to the current council is establishing that the complaint was made and not actioned — because the council's own records will not show it.

Emergent Pattern 3 — The Grant Eligibility Lock

Surfaces generating this pattern: Mesh 2 (institutional non-response — no grant application documented) and Mesh 3 (legislative architecture — grant eligibility requires registered camp; camp registration not confirmed).

What this pattern reveals that path-level analysis cannot: The grant access failure and the camp registration failure are not independent. They are structurally linked. The NSW Flying-Fox Camp Management Grant Program requires a registered camp. If the Taylors Arm camp is not registered in the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program, the eligibility condition cannot be met. The council cannot access the grant without first notifying the camp to the monitoring program. The notification mechanism exists: speciespolicy@environment.gov.au and CSIRO contacts David Westcott at david.westcott@csiro.au and Adam McKeown at adam.mckeown@csiro.au. The step is available. It has not been taken. This means the budget constraint Kempsey Shire cited as the reason for denying the Bellbrook request may itself be addressable — if the camp is registered and a grant application is made. The path from complaint to funded response is blocked not by law but by an administrative step that has not been initiated.

Structural significance: HIGH. This is the most actionable finding in the Lattice Atlas. Notification of the camp to the monitoring program is a single administrative action. It does not require council cooperation. The community, the investigator, or an advocate can notify CSIRO directly. Upon registration, grant eligibility may be established.

Emergent Pattern 4 — The Corridor Duplication

Surfaces generating this pattern: Mesh 1 (community harm — Taylors Arm) and Mesh 2 (institutional non-response — Nambucca Valley) cross-referenced against the Bellbrook and Kempsey Shire evidence in the same register (EVID-015, EVID-016).

What this pattern reveals that path-level analysis cannot: Two communities in the same hinterland river corridor, under two different councils, with two different histories, have arrived at structurally identical positions: active colony, documented community harm, council non-response or budget-denial, no management plan, no grant access, no Commonwealth monitoring registration confirmed. The duplication is not explained by geography alone. It is produced by the same legislative framework (discretionary camp management policy, mandatory biosecurity obligation not enforced, dual-jurisdiction referral threshold creating structural hesitation) operating identically across two jurisdictions. This is not two local failures. This is a regional governance architecture producing identical outputs across adjacent jurisdictions.

Structural significance: VERY HIGH. The corridor duplication converts two isolated community complaints into a systemic finding. A submission that documents both communities together — same species, same corridor, same failure pattern, same elected representation, same legislative framework — carries structural weight that neither case carries alone. This is the basis for a cross-community submission to the NSW Environment Minister or a Senate committee.

Emergent Pattern 5 — The Electoral Silence Convergence

Surfaces generating this pattern: Mesh 2 (institutional non-response — council) and Mesh 3 (governance architecture — electoral representation, EVID-019, EVID-020).

What this pattern reveals that path-level analysis cannot: The same National Party representation covers both communities at state (Michael Kemp, Oxley) and federal (Pat Conaghan, Cowper) levels. Both members represent rural communities experiencing wildlife management failures. Neither has documented public advocacy on the flying fox issue for either community across the entire documented period. This is not a finding about individuals. It is a structural observation: the political representation layer — which is one of the accountability pathways available to communities that cannot get council response — is silent. The community has a council that does not respond and representatives who have not publicly advocated. Both layers have failed simultaneously. The corridor duplication means this convergent silence applies to both communities in both LGAs within the same electorate boundaries.

Structural significance: MEDIUM-HIGH. The electoral silence is documented as a structural absence. It becomes an input to the Temporal Projection Integrity Engine for forward classification.

Stable at zero. No documented movement from either state or federal member toward public advocacy on this issue.

Convergence assessment

All indicators for which directionality can be assessed — council responsiveness, grant access, electoral advocacy — move in the same direction: away from resolution and toward institutional embedding of the failure. No countervailing forces are currently operative in the evidence. Where all indicators move in the same direction, the convergence must be named explicitly: the documented trajectory is toward a permanent governance gap in which a 133-person village with no reticulated water, a nationally significant tourism asset, and an active grey-headed flying fox colony continues without council management response, without grant access, and without electoral advocacy indefinitely.
