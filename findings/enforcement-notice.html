PART ONE — IDENTIFICATION OF HARM

1.1 Subject Community Taylors Arm is a village of 133 persons (ABS 2021 Census) located within Nambucca Valley Local Government Area, New South Wales. The village does not have access to reticulated town water. Residents rely on rainwater harvesting systems. The primary economic asset of the village is the Pub With No Beer, a nationally recognised tourism destination. The Taylors Arm Sports Reserve, located directly opposite the Pub With No Beer, is managed by Nambucca Valley Council.

1.2 The Colony A colony of grey-headed flying foxes (Pteropus poliocephalus) is established in the village of Taylors Arm. The grey-headed flying fox is listed as Vulnerable under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (Cth) and Vulnerable in New South Wales under the Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016. At the March 2023 public forum, the colony was described as having more than doubled in size that year. The colony is roosting in Camphor Laurel trees (Cinnamomum camphora) at the rear of residential properties.

1.3 Documented Harm The following categories of harm are recorded in verbatim form in Nambucca Valley Council Ordinary Meeting Minutes, 16 March 2023 (Evidence Register EVID-006): — Tank drinking water quality contamination from faecal matter of the roosting colony. — Soiled washing from faecal matter deposited on residential clotheslines. — Foul odour affecting residential amenity and outdoor use of private property. — Vehicle paintwork damage from faecal matter. — Loss of usable outdoor space, including inability to allow children to play outdoors.

The Bellbrook precedent, documented in Evidence Register EVID-016, establishes that equivalent colony conditions in an adjacent hinterland village produced loss of hospitality trade and tourism impact. The Taylors Arm Sports Reserve campground and the Pub With No Beer present an identical commercial risk profile.

1.4 The Specific Remedy Request Made On 16 March 2023, Vicki Provost addressed Nambucca Valley Council at a public forum held at Taylors Arm Hall and asked whether anything could be done by council regarding removal of the Camphor Laurel trees in which the colony is roosting. This request is recorded verbatim in the council minutes (Evidence Register EVID-006). No response to this request has been documented in any Nambucca Valley Council record across the period March 2023 to April 2026.

PART THREE — LEGISLATIVE BASIS FOR OBLIGATIONS

3.1 Nambucca Valley Council — Biosecurity Act 2015 (NSW) Camphor Laurel (Cinnamomum camphora) is a priority weed under the Biosecurity Act 2015 and the North Coast Regional Strategic Weed Management Plan 2023-2027 (EVID-012, EVID-033 through EVID-037). Under section 371 of the Biosecurity Act 2015, local control authorities hold weed control functions including preventing, eliminating, minimising and managing weed biosecurity risk; developing, implementing, coordinating and reviewing weed control programs; inspecting land; and keeping records (EVID-035). Nambucca Valley Council's own Roadside Vegetation Management Policy ES-16 states that council as Local Control Authority has a legal requirement under the Biosecurity Act to actively control and reduce the spread of Camphor Laurel (EVID-010). Nambucca Valley Council's own Weeds page states authorised officers carry out regular property inspections and enforce control requirements as prescribed within the Biosecurity Act 2015 and the North Coast Regional Strategic Plan (EVID-011).

3.2 Nambucca Valley Council — Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015 (NSW) The Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015 strongly encourages local councils and other land managers to prepare camp management plans for sites where the local community is affected (EVID-070). The policy provides a framework within which the Office of Environment and Heritage makes regulatory decisions. The Flying-Fox Camp Management Code of Practice 2018 states that camp managers should prepare a camp management plan in consultation with affected communities for all flying-fox camp management actions, and that a flying-fox camp management plan template is available from the Department (EVID-071, EVID-072).

3.3 Nambucca Valley Council — Local Government Act 1993 (NSW) Nambucca Valley Council's own Community Strategic Plan lists Public Health — Clean Water and Local Economy as key strategic directions. The community's documented harm includes tank drinking water quality contamination and commercial tourism impact. The council's own stated obligations under its Community Strategic Plan have not been activated in response to the documented harm.

3.4 NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water — Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015 The NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water administers the Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015. The department provides expert advice to assist councils in preparing camp management plans and guides them through the necessary processes. The department coordinates the NSW contribution to the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program, with the NSW participation contact documented as ff.census@environment.nsw.gov.au (EVID-048).

3.5 Commonwealth Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water — Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 The grey-headed flying fox is listed as Vulnerable under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (EVID-065). The species is assessed as a single national population. The Commonwealth Department administers the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program in coordination with CSIRO and state governments (EVID-042). The program counts camps at all known locations and acknowledges that camps may not be known to the monitoring program (EVID-049). The mechanism for notification of unregistered camps is documented as speciespolicy@environment.gov.au (EVID-021). Camp data is now accessible through the CSIRO Data Access Portal following decommissioning of the web viewer on 25 June 2025 (EVID-038, EVID-039). The National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program is currently under review (EVID-045).

PART FIVE — REMEDY DEMANDS

5.1 Remedy Demands Addressed to Nambucca Valley Council Self-Remediation Classification: Available. The following remedy demands are addressed to Nambucca Valley Council as a body with both the obligation and the structural capacity to act.

Remedy NVC-1: Nambucca Valley Council acknowledges receipt of the public forum presentation made by Vicki Provost on 16 March 2023 regarding the flying fox infestation at Taylors Arm Village, and confirms in writing to the Taylors Arm community what action, if any, was taken in response to that presentation and what action, if any, is now proposed.

Remedy NVC-2: Nambucca Valley Council notifies the Taylors Arm flying fox camp to the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program by contacting Dr David Westcott (david.westcott@csiro.au) or Mr Adam McKeown (adam.mckeown@csiro.au) at CSIRO, or by emailing speciespolicy@environment.gov.au, with a description of the camp location, estimated size, species composition, and duration of occupation, consistent with the notification mechanism documented at EVID-021 and EVID-059.

Remedy NVC-3: Nambucca Valley Council commences a Review of Environmental Factors under the Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979 for the Camphor Laurel trees at Taylors Arm in which the colony is roosting, engaging a qualified ecologist, consistent with the process conducted by Kempsey Shire Council in April 2024 (EVID-014), and communicates the outcome of that review to the Taylors Arm community.

Remedy NVC-4: Nambucca Valley Council applies for available grant funding under the NSW Flying-Fox Camp Management Grant Program or the LGNSW Flying-Fox Habitat Restoration Program upon confirmation of camp registration, and informs the Taylors Arm community of the outcome of any application.

Remedy NVC-5: Nambucca Valley Council develops a flying fox camp management plan for the Taylors Arm site, consistent with its obligation under the Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015 and the Flying-Fox Camp Management Code of Practice 2018, using the camp management plan template available from NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water, in consultation with the affected community.

5.2 Remedy Demands Addressed to NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water Self-Remediation Classification: Available. The following remedy demands are addressed to the NSW Department as administrator of the Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015.

Remedy NSW-1: The NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water provides direct advice and support to Nambucca Valley Council on the process for notifying the Taylors Arm flying fox camp to the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program and for developing a camp management plan, consistent with the department's documented function of providing expert advice to assist councils (EVID-070, EVID-071).

Remedy NSW-2: The NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water confirms in writing whether the Taylors Arm flying fox camp is currently registered in the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program or any NSW Environment camp database, and if not, initiates the registration process consistent with NSW's responsibility for coordinating flying fox counts in the state (EVID-047, EVID-048).

Remedy NSW-3: The NSW Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water confirms the current status of flying fox grant programs available to NSW councils for hinterland camp management, and advises Nambucca Valley Council of the steps required to access those programs for the Taylors Arm site.

5.3 Remedy Demands Addressed to Commonwealth Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water Self-Remediation Classification: Constrained — the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program is currently under review and the web viewer was decommissioned on 25 June 2025. Remedy CWLTH-1: The Commonwealth Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water confirms in writing whether the Taylors Arm flying fox camp is known to the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program, and if not, ensures that the camp is assessed for inclusion in the monitoring architecture that succeeds the decommissioned web viewer upon completion of the current program review, consistent with the Commonwealth's obligation to monitor the national population of a Commonwealth-listed vulnerable species (EVID-038, EVID-045, EVID-065).

PART SIX — REGIONAL CORRIDOR FINDING

This Enforcement Notice documents a structural failure that is not confined to Taylors Arm. The Lattice Atlas Emergent Pattern 4 (Corridor Duplication) establishes that an equivalent failure is simultaneously documented at Bellbrook, Kempsey Shire Local Government Area.

Bellbrook: grey-headed flying fox colony active since November 2019 following Black Summer fires. Kempsey Shire Council lists Bellbrook as a historic camp despite six years of active occupation. A formal management plan request made in May 2025 was denied on budget grounds. No management plan exists for the Bellbrook site (EVID-015, EVID-016).

The two communities sit in the same hinterland river corridor. Both sit within NSW state electorate of Oxley (Michael Kemp, Nationals) and federal electorate of Cowper (Pat Conaghan, Nationals). Both have grey-headed flying fox colonies without management plans. Both have no documented access to available grant programs for the affected communities. Both have council records that either do not document the camp as active (Bellbrook: listed as historic) or do not document any response to the community complaint (Taylors Arm: no Outstanding Actions entry).

The same legislative framework, the same discretionary camp management policy, the same mandatory biosecurity obligation not activated, and the same dual-jurisdiction referral threshold are producing identical outcomes across two adjacent jurisdictions simultaneously.

All recipients of this notice are directed to consider the Taylors Arm findings in the context of this regional corridor pattern. A cross-corridor submission to the NSW Environment Minister and to a relevant parliamentary committee is available as a Classification 2 enforcement pathway (Observation F, Temporal Projection Integrity Engine gate record).
