EVID-013

Source: Macleay Argus, 19 July 2017, story ID 4800875, nambuccaguardian.com.au related story links confirmed.

Record: Nambucca Shire Council (now Nambucca Valley Council) developed a flying fox camp management plan for Gordon Park, Nambucca Heads in 2017 using a $15,000 state government grant and $19,200 council contribution. The plan was developed with EcoSURE consultants and required approval by the NSW Office of Environment and Heritage.

EVID-014

Source: Kempsey Shire Council news release, 5 April 2024, kempsey.nsw.gov.au.

Record: Kempsey Shire Council announced removal of Camphor Laurel trees across the shire, noting that well-established camphor laurels cannot be removed without approval under the Tree Preservation Order due to the habitat and food source they provide to animals. Council conducted a Review of Environmental Factors before proceeding and engaged a local ecologist.

EVID-030

Source: Ecosure Environmental Consultancy, ecosure.com.au/flying-fox-nsw-grants-program/, confirmed.

It is a recurring program administered by LGNSW. Grants are available for developing management plans and implementing camp management actions. Eligibility requires councils to have a registered camp.

EVID-031

Campbelltown Council received $348,086 under this program for Bingara Reserve. This program is distinct from the camp management grant program and operates in parallel.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-fox camps can occur in trees close to houses and livestock and that residents living near camps often have concerns regarding noise, damage to vegetation and hygiene.

EVID-046

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program section.

Record: DCCEEW states the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program focuses primarily on monitoring national Grey-headed Flying-fox and Spectacled Flying-fox populations; within the range of those two species, counts of Black Flying-foxes and Little Red Flying-foxes are also undertaken.

EVID-047

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Who is doing the counting?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states participating state and territory environment departments coordinate counts in their jurisdictions by identifying camps, maintaining a database of their locations, and assigning counters for those camps.

EVID-048

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Who is doing the counting?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states New South Wales flying-fox count participation contact is ff.census@environment.nsw.gov.au.

EVID-049

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Is it possible to count all flying-fox camps?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states there are always going to be camps and flying-foxes that are missed because camps may not be known to anyone, may not be known to the monitoring program, or may not be practical or feasible to count.

EVID-050

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Why monitor?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states monitoring flying-foxes is important because grey-headed flying-foxes and spectacled flying-foxes are listed as threatened under the Commonwealth EPBC Act and relevant state legislation, and that monitoring results help inform responses to public concerns about impacts on industry, agriculture and public health.

EVID-051

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "How is the monitoring being conducted?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program is designed to collect data on abundance and distribution of flying-foxes in eastern Australia.

EVID-052

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "How is the monitoring being conducted?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states counts will be conducted at all known camps in the range of the grey-headed flying-fox, which stretches from Adelaide through to Bundaberg, and the spectacled flying-fox, which is between Ingham and Cooktown in far north Queensland.

EVID-053

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "How is the monitoring being conducted?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states the large size of flying-fox aggregations, or camps, and their extreme mobility mean flying-fox monitoring is not a straightforward task.

EVID-054

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "How is the monitoring being conducted?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states that at small camps, usually 1000 individuals or fewer, flying-foxes are counted directly.

EVID-055

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "How is the monitoring being conducted?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states that at larger camps it is not possible to count every flying-fox, requiring a sampling method.

EVID-056

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Is it possible to count all flying-fox camps?" section.

Record: DCCEEW states all possible efforts will be made to include and count all known camps, but this may not always be possible.

EVID-057

Source: DCCEEW, Monitoring Flying-Fox Populations page, "Reports of the National Flying-fox Monitoring Program" section.

Record: DCCEEW states reports of counts under the National Flying-Fox Monitoring Program are published once the data has been checked and analysed.

EVID-060

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-foxes generally congregate in camps made up of large numbers of individuals, but some also roost singly or in small groups.

EVID-061

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-fox camps can be found in a range of vegetation types, usually close to water in an area with a dense understorey.

EVID-062

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-foxes are highly mobile, ranging up to 40 kilometres from their camps at night to feed.

EVID-063

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-foxes may move up to hundreds of kilometres to follow flowering and fruiting food sources.

EVID-064

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states flying-foxes pollinate flowers and disperse seeds as they forage on nectar, pollen, and fruits.

EVID-065

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states the Grey-headed Flying-fox, Pteropus poliocephalus, is listed as Vulnerable under the EPBC Act and Vulnerable in New South Wales.

EVID-066

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states the Grey-headed Flying-fox occurs in the coastal belt from Rockhampton in central Queensland to Adelaide in South Australia.

EVID-067

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states state governments, irrespective of national listing status, consider all species of flying-fox to be protected native species.

EVID-068

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states activities likely to have a significant impact on the Grey-headed, Spectacled, or Christmas Island Flying-fox must be referred to the Australian Government.

EVID-069

Source: DCCEEW, Flying-foxes and national environmental law page, last updated 25 September 2023.

Record: DCCEEW states activities that may require referral include proposals to disperse camps, move or shift camp boundaries, or clear important roosting or foraging habitat.

EVID-070

Source: NSW Environment and Heritage, Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015, environment.nsw.gov.au/node/33221, retrieved June 2026.

Record: The policy states it strongly encourages local councils and other land managers to prepare camp management plans for sites where the local community is affected. It is not mandatory — it is strongly encouraged. The policy provides a framework within which the Office of Environment and Heritage makes regulatory decisions.

EVID-071

Source: NSW Environment and Heritage, Flying-fox camp management plans page, environment.nsw.gov.au/node/21406, retrieved June 2026.

Record: NSW DCCEEW states land managers should develop a camp management plan to record how a flying-fox camp or a number of camps will be managed. Recent plans developed by land managers have covered the entire local government area. A flying-fox camp management plan template is available from the Department. The template meets the requirements of the Flying-Fox Camp Management Code of Practice 2018 and Flying-Fox Camp Management Policy 2015.

EVID-072

Source: NSW Environment and Heritage, Flying-fox Camp Management Code of Practice 2018, environment.nsw.gov.au/node/21411, retrieved June 2026.

Record: Camp managers should prepare a camp management plan in consultation with affected communities for all flying-fox camp management actions. Some high impact and high-risk actions under the Code will require the development of a camp management plan. Camp management action at nationally important flying-fox camps may require referral to the Australian Government under the EPBC Act. NSW DCCEEW states understorey weeds can provide important roosting habitat for flying-foxes, especially in heat stress events.
